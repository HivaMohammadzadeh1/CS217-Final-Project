\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has become the standard approach for aligning large language models with human preferences, but its iterative training loop remains computationally expensive.
Unlike inference or supervised fine-tuning, RLHF cycles through three distinct phases---policy rollouts, reward scoring, and PPO gradient updates---each with different numerical precision requirements.
We observe that current hardware accelerators miss optimization opportunities by applying uniform precision across all operations.

This paper explores layer-adaptive quantization using Microscaling (MX) formats to reduce FPGA energy consumption during RLHF training.
Working with Qwen2.5-0.5B on the Anthropic HH-RLHF dataset, we first characterize GEMM performance on an AWS F2 FPGA, revealing that 99.99\% of per-operation latency stems from PCIe data transfer rather than computation---a bottleneck that data compression directly addresses.
We then profile all 169 linear layers under MXFP4 and MXFP8 quantization, finding that approximately 80\% tolerate aggressive 4-bit quantization with negligible quality loss, while sensitive layers concentrate at network boundaries.

Based on these findings, we define four quantization policies ranging from conservative (MXFP8 everywhere) to phase-adaptive (format varies by RLHF phase) and implement a dual-precision processing element in SystemC and HLS.
Our analysis projects 20--25\% energy savings for bandwidth-dominated FPGA deployments, demonstrating that exploiting the inherent precision heterogeneity of RLHF workloads offers a practical path toward more efficient alignment training.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

As large language models move from research to production, post-training alignment via RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training} has become essential for ensuring safe and helpful behavior.
What began in frontier AI labs is now spreading to enterprises fine-tuning models for legal analysis, medical question answering, financial advice, and customer support.
This proliferation brings new urgency to reducing the computational cost of RLHF, which remains substantially more expensive than supervised fine-tuning.

The source of this expense lies in RLHF's iterative structure.
Each training step cycles through three phases: the policy model generates candidate responses (rollout), a reward model scores them, and gradients flow backward to update parameters via PPO.
Matrix multiplications dominate all three phases, accounting for roughly 80--90\% of compute.
Yet these phases have fundamentally different precision requirements.
Rollouts need only sample ``good enough'' tokens---small numerical errors wash out in the sampling process.
Reward scoring compresses intermediate representations to a single scalar, providing natural noise robustness.
Gradient updates, however, accumulate small parameter changes across many samples, making them sensitive to quantization error.

Current accelerators ignore this heterogeneity.
Whether using FP16 on GPUs or INT8 on FPGAs, they apply uniform precision across all operations, leaving potential energy savings unrealized.
The Microscaling (MX) format family~\citep{rouhani2023mxformat} offers a path forward.
MX formats use \emph{group scaling}---a shared exponent governs blocks of values, preserving dynamic range while reducing per-element bit width.
MXFP8 matches FP16 quality on most workloads; MXFP4 halves storage at the cost of reduced precision suitable for noise-tolerant computations.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/rlhf_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{The three phases of RLHF training and their precision tolerance. Rollouts tolerate aggressive quantization (MXFP4); gradient updates require higher precision (FP16/MXFP8).}
\label{fig:rlhf-pipeline}
\end{figure}

This paper asks whether the phase-specific and layer-specific precision tolerance of RLHF can be exploited to reduce FPGA energy consumption through adaptive MX format assignment.
Our investigation proceeds in three stages.
First, we establish an INT8 baseline on AWS F2 (Xilinx VU9P), measuring cycle-level performance for GEMM offload.
Second, we profile all 169 linear layers in Qwen2.5-0.5B under MXFP4 and MXFP8 quantization, identifying which layers tolerate aggressive compression.
Third, we define quantization policies that assign precision per layer and per RLHF phase, implementing the corresponding dual-precision datapath in SystemC and HLS.

Our main findings are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item PCIe data transfer accounts for 99.99\% of per-matmul latency on our FPGA baseline, making bandwidth compression the primary lever for energy reduction.
    \item Approximately 80\% of layers tolerate MXFP4 quantization with less than 2\% perplexity impact; sensitive layers concentrate at network boundaries (early embedding transformations and late output shaping).
    \item A balanced policy applying MXFP4 to tolerant layers during rollout and reward phases projects 20\% energy savings with negligible quality degradation.
    \item Our dual-precision PE supports runtime mode switching between MXFP4, MXFP8, and INT8, enabling flexible policy deployment without FPGA reconfiguration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{RLHF Training}
Reinforcement learning from human feedback~\citep{ouyang2022training, christiano2017deep} has emerged as the dominant paradigm for aligning language models with human preferences.
The approach first trains a reward model on pairwise preference data, then uses this reward signal to optimize the policy via Proximal Policy Optimization~\citep{schulman2017ppo}.
Each PPO iteration generates responses from the current policy, scores them with the reward model, and computes gradients to improve future generations.
The TRL library~\citep{vonwerra2022trl} provides the standard PyTorch implementation we build upon.

\paragraph{Microscaling Formats}
The MX format family~\citep{rouhani2023mxformat} introduces block-scaled numerical representations designed for deep learning workloads.
Rather than storing a full exponent per element, MX formats share a single scale factor across groups of $k$ values (typically 8 or 16), reducing storage while preserving dynamic range.
MXFP8 uses 8-bit elements (4-bit exponent, 3-bit mantissa) and matches FP16 quality on most inference tasks.
MXFP4 uses 4-bit elements (2-bit exponent, 1-bit mantissa), halving bandwidth at the cost of reduced precision.
Figure~\ref{fig:mx-formats} illustrates the bit layouts.
Microsoft's \texttt{mx-pytorch} library provides reference implementations for quantization and dequantization.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/mx_format_comparison.pdf}
\caption{Bit layouts for INT8, MXFP8, and MXFP4. MX formats share a group scale factor across multiple elements, enabling aggressive bit-width reduction while maintaining dynamic range.}
\label{fig:mx-formats}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Low-Precision Training}
Mixed-precision training with FP16~\citep{micikevicius2018mixed} is now standard practice, and recent work extends this to FP8~\citep{micikevicius2022fp8}.
NVIDIA's H100 provides native FP8 tensor cores, demonstrating industry momentum toward lower precision.
However, sub-8-bit training remains challenging because gradient accumulation amplifies quantization errors.
Our work sidesteps this limitation by applying aggressive quantization selectively---only to phases and layers that can tolerate it.

\paragraph{FPGA Acceleration}
FPGAs offer energy-efficient matrix multiplication through custom datapaths and flexible precision~\citep{guo2017angel, wang2021fpga}.
The AWS F2 platform provides cloud-accessible Xilinx VU9P FPGAs with PCIe Gen3 x16 connectivity.
We extend the CS217 Lab~1 INT8 processing element with MX format support.

\paragraph{Adaptive Quantization}
Prior work on mixed-precision quantization~\citep{dong2019hawq, wang2019haq} uses Hessian eigenvalues or learned policies to assign per-layer bit widths for inference.
Our work differs in three ways: we target RLHF training rather than inference, we exploit phase-specific tolerance within the training loop, and we use MX group scaling rather than per-tensor quantization.

\section{Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{System Overview}

Our system partitions RLHF training between a host CPU and an FPGA accelerator.
The host handles high-level training logic---tokenization, sampling, KL divergence computation, and PPO updates---using PyTorch and the TRL library.
All matrix multiplications are intercepted and offloaded to the FPGA through a tiling layer that decomposes arbitrary matrix dimensions into 16$\times$16 tiles, transfers them via AXI, and reassembles the results.
An adaptive controller consults a per-layer quantization policy and configures the FPGA's precision mode before each operation.
Figure~\ref{fig:system-overview} illustrates the architecture.

\subsection{Model and Dataset}

We use Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct~\citep{qwen2025qwen25} as both policy and reward model.
This 0.5B-parameter transformer has 24 layers, each containing seven linear projections: Q, K, V, and O in attention, plus gate, up, and down in the MLP.
Including the language modeling head, this yields 169 quantizable layers---sufficient to study layer-wise sensitivity patterns while remaining tractable for exhaustive profiling.

For training data, we curate a 1,200-sample subset of Anthropic HH-RLHF~\citep{bai2022training}, split into 1,000 training and 200 evaluation pairs with sequences capped at 512 tokens.
We train for 50 PPO steps with batch size 8.
Each forward pass requires 170 matrix multiplications ($24 \times 7 + 2$ for the heads), and including the backward pass, the full training run executes approximately 136,000 FPGA matmul operations.


\subsection{INT8 FPGA Baseline}
\label{sec:fpga-baseline}

We deploy the CS217 Lab~1 matrix multiply accelerator on AWS F2, which provides a Xilinx VU9P running at 250~MHz with estimated power draw of 35W.
The accelerator computes 16$\times$16 INT8 matrix multiplications, with each operation transferring 336 bytes over PCIe: 256 bytes for weights, 16 bytes for the input vector, and 64 bytes for the output.

To characterize performance, we average cycle counts over 10 iterations, separating data transfer cycles (PCIe round-trips) from compute cycles (on-chip MAC operations).
Energy estimates use $E = P \times T$ with $P = 35$W from Xilinx Power Estimator and $T$ measured as wall-clock time.


\subsection{Layer Sensitivity Profiling}
\label{sec:sensitivity-profiling}

To determine which layers tolerate aggressive quantization, we profile each of the 169 linear layers independently using Microsoft's \texttt{mx-pytorch} library.
For each layer $\ell$, we quantize only that layer to the target format (MXFP4 or MXFP8, with group size 8 or 16) while keeping all other layers at FP16.
We then evaluate perplexity on a held-out calibration set and compute the relative change:
$\Delta_\ell = (\text{PPL}_\ell - \text{PPL}_{\text{base}}) / \text{PPL}_{\text{base}} \times 100$.

We classify a layer as MXFP4-tolerant if $|\Delta_\ell| < 2\%$ across all group sizes tested.
The complete profiling produces a sensitivity matrix with layers as rows, format-group combinations as columns, and perplexity deltas as entries.


\subsection{Quantization Policies}
\label{sec:policies}

The sensitivity matrix guides our definition of four quantization policies, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:policies}.
Each policy assigns precision per layer and per RLHF phase, reflecting different tradeoffs between energy savings and quality preservation.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Policy} & \textbf{Rollout} & \textbf{Reward} & \textbf{Gradient} \\
\midrule
A (Conservative)   & MXFP8 all   & MXFP8 all   & FP16 all \\
B (Balanced)       & MXFP4/8 adaptive & MXFP4/8 adaptive & FP16 all \\
C (Aggressive)     & MXFP4 all   & MXFP4 all   & MXFP4/8 adaptive \\
D (Phase-Adaptive) & MXFP4 tolerant & MXFP8 all & MXFP8/FP16 adaptive \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Quantization policies for RLHF phases. ``Adaptive'' means MXFP4 for tolerant layers, higher precision for sensitive ones. All policies use group size $k{=}8$.}
\label{tab:policies}
\end{table}

Policy~B represents our primary recommendation.
It applies MXFP4 to the roughly 80\% of layers that tolerate it during rollout and reward phases, achieving substantial bandwidth reduction without quality degradation, while preserving FP16 precision for gradient updates where accumulation errors matter most.
Policy~D goes further by varying precision across phases: aggressive quantization during rollouts (where sampling absorbs numerical noise), moderate precision for reward scoring (which shapes the gradient signal), and conservative precision for gradient computation.


\subsection{Hardware Design}
\label{sec:hardware}

\subsubsection{SystemC Reference Model}

Before synthesizing hardware, we develop a cycle-accurate SystemC reference model for the dual-precision datapath.
The model comprises four components: type definitions for precision modes and minifloat formats (\texttt{mx\_types.h}), group scaling logic that finds the maximum exponent within blocks of $k$ values (\texttt{group\_scaler.h}), a processing element that performs MX-encoded multiply-accumulate (\texttt{mxfp\_pe.h}), and a datapath controller that orchestrates 16$\times$16 GEMMs with runtime mode switching (\texttt{mx\_datapath.h}).

The group scaler implements both quantization (clamping mantissas to target width) and dequantization (reconstructing values from element bits plus shared scale).
Testbenches verify bit-exact agreement with PyTorch reference outputs from \texttt{mx-pytorch}.

\subsubsection{HLS Datapath}

The HLS implementation extends the Lab~1 INT8 core with MX format decoding.
A \texttt{DecodeMXByte} function extracts sign, exponent, and mantissa from MXFP8 or MXFP4 encoded bytes, applies the group scale, and produces INT16 fixed-point values compatible with the existing MAC pipeline.
The host configures precision mode via an AXI control register before each GEMM tile, and the original INT8 path remains as a fallback.

This design enables zero-overhead mode switching on a per-tile basis.
The adaptive controller can implement any of the four quantization policies without FPGA reconfiguration, simply by writing the appropriate mode before each operation.

\subsection{INT8 FPGA Baseline}

Table~\ref{tab:fpga-baseline} summarizes our baseline measurements on AWS F2.
The most striking finding is the extreme imbalance between transfer and compute: on-chip computation requires only 15 cycles, while data transfer consumes 148,641 cycles---a ratio of nearly 10,000:1.

\bottomrule

\label{tab:fpga-baseline}

The FPGA is not compute-bound; it is overwhelmingly memory-bandwidth-bound.
Ideal PCIe Gen3 x16 bandwidth of roughly 12~GB/s predicts 28~$\mu$s transfer time for 336 bytes, but we observe 594~$\mu$s---20$\times$ worse---due to AXI protocol overhead, host memory copies, and SRAM serialization latencies.

This bottleneck directly motivates data compression.
MXFP4 halves the weight payload from 256 to 128 bytes (plus a small group-scale overhead), yielding near-proportional reduction in the dominant cost component.
Figure~\ref{fig:cycle-breakdown} visualizes the cycle breakdown.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Understanding the PCIe Bottleneck}

The 99.99\% PCIe overhead we observe warrants further examination.
Each 16$\times$16 operation transfers 336 bytes and computes in 15 cycles (60~ns at 250~MHz), yet the round-trip takes 594~$\mu$s---nearly 10,000$\times$ longer than computation.

This finding has three implications for FPGA-accelerated ML beyond our specific project:

\textbf{Compression dominates compute optimization.}
For small-tile offload architectures, reducing data volume yields far greater returns than faster arithmetic.
Our MX approach directly exploits this by halving weight precision.

\textbf{Batching amortizes overhead.}
Larger tiles spread fixed per-transfer latency across more useful compute.
A 64$\times$64 tile would increase compute from 15 to roughly 960 cycles while only quadrupling transfer volume, dramatically improving the compute-to-transfer ratio.

\textbf{Weight caching eliminates redundant transfers.}
Keeping weights in FPGA SRAM between operations would remove repeated transfers for the same layer, though this requires sufficient on-chip storage for the working set.


\subsection{Architectural Patterns in Sensitivity}

The sensitivity matrix reveals a consistent architectural pattern: attention layers tolerate aggressive quantization universally, while MLP sensitivity concentrates at network boundaries.

Early blocks (2--3) transform embedding-space representations into the model's internal feature space---a large dynamic range that MXFP4's 2-bit exponent struggles to capture.
Late blocks (21--23) shape the output logit distribution, similarly requiring precision to distinguish between tokens.
Middle blocks (4--20) perform gradual feature refinement within established ranges, making them MXFP4-tolerant.

Group size has minimal impact: switching from $k{=}8$ to $k{=}16$ slightly increases perplexity deltas but does not change which layers are classified as tolerant.
This validates our choice of $k{=}8$, where the modest overhead of more frequent group scales is justified by better precision.


\subsection{Phase-Specific Precision Requirements}

Each RLHF phase has distinct precision needs arising from how numerical errors propagate:

\textbf{Rollout} phases are most tolerant because autoregressive generation samples from a softmax distribution.
Temperature scaling and top-$k$/top-$p$ filtering absorb small logit perturbations.
MXFP4 is safe for all layers.

\textbf{Reward scoring} offers moderate tolerance.
The reward model compresses all intermediate computations to a single scalar score per response, naturally smoothing over small numerical variations.
MXFP8 is universally safe; MXFP4 works for tolerant layers.

\textbf{Gradient updates} require the most care.
PPO gradients accumulate over many samples, and quantization noise compounds across training steps.
FP16 or MXFP8 is recommended; MXFP4 risks training instability except for the most tolerant layers.

This hierarchy motivates Policy~D, which maximizes compression during rollouts, applies moderate quantization for rewards, and preserves precision for gradients.


\subsection{Comparison with GPU Baselines}

Our FPGA measurements provide a controlled research environment for studying precision trade-offs, but production GPU deployments differ substantially.
NVIDIA H100 provides native FP8 tensor cores and HBM3 bandwidth ($\sim$3~TB/s), making the transfer bottleneck far less pronounced than PCIe Gen3.
However, H100's 700W power draw is 20$\times$ our FPGA's 35W, making energy comparisons nuanced.

The FPGA platform enables measurement granularity not feasible on GPUs, where power monitoring cannot attribute energy to individual operations.
Our sensitivity patterns should transfer to GPU deployments, though the absolute savings will depend on the memory-compute balance of each platform.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Practical Deployment Strategy}

Our results suggest a staged deployment approach for RLHF energy optimization:

\textbf{Profile once, deploy everywhere.}
The sensitivity matrix depends on model architecture, not training data.
Profile the model once on a small calibration set, then apply the resulting policy across all RLHF runs with that architecture.

\textbf{Start conservatively, then optimize.}
Begin with Policy~A (MXFP8 everywhere), which is universally safe with less than 0.16\% quality impact.
After validating training stability, graduate to Policy~B for the roughly 80\% of layers that tolerate MXFP4, achieving 20\% energy reduction with negligible quality risk.

\textbf{Exploit phase structure for maximum savings.}
Policy~D takes full advantage of RLHF's inherent precision heterogeneity, but requires runtime mode switching.
Our HLS design supports this, enabling per-operation precision selection without FPGA reconfiguration.


\subsection{Limitations}

Several factors limit the generality of our findings:

\textbf{Model scale.}
Qwen2.5-0.5B is small by current standards.
Larger models may show different sensitivity patterns, though the attention-tolerant/MLP-boundary-sensitive pattern has architectural grounding that should transfer.

\textbf{Measurement platform.}
The 99.99\% transfer overhead reflects our 16$\times$16 tile size and single-operation offload.
Batched transfers or on-chip weight caching would shift the energy breakdown, potentially reducing the relative benefit of compression.

\textbf{Training duration.}
We run 50 PPO steps, sufficient to demonstrate the approach but not production-scale alignment.
Longer runs are needed to verify that quantization noise does not accumulate over thousands of steps.

\textbf{Projection vs.\ measurement.}
Our MX energy estimates extrapolate from INT8 cycle counts rather than direct FPGA measurement with the MX datapath deployed.

\textbf{Architecture scope.}
Sensitivity patterns may differ for architectures with mixture-of-experts, different attention variants, or non-standard FFN structures.


% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We investigated layer-adaptive MX format quantization for reducing FPGA energy consumption during RLHF training.
Our baseline measurements reveal that PCIe data transfer dominates per-operation latency (99.99\% of cycles), making bandwidth compression the primary optimization lever.
Profiling all 169 layers in Qwen2.5-0.5B shows that approximately 80\% tolerate MXFP4 quantization with less than 2\% perplexity impact, while sensitive layers concentrate at network boundaries where dynamic range requirements are highest.
MXFP8 is universally safe with maximum 0.16\% perplexity change.

Based on these findings, we define four quantization policies that balance energy savings against quality preservation.
Our recommended Policy~B applies MXFP4 to tolerant layers during rollout and reward phases, projecting 20\% energy reduction with negligible quality risk.
The dual-precision HLS datapath we developed supports runtime mode switching, enabling flexible policy deployment without FPGA reconfiguration.

\paragraph{Future Directions}
This work opens several paths for continued research.
First, synthesizing and deploying the MX datapath on FPGA would replace our cycle-count projections with direct energy measurements.
Second, validating sensitivity patterns on larger models (7B+ parameters) would establish whether the architectural patterns we observe generalize.
Third, extending training to thousands of PPO steps would verify long-term stability under MX quantization.
Finally, implementing on-chip weight caching would further reduce the transfer overhead that motivates compression, potentially enabling even more aggressive optimizations.
