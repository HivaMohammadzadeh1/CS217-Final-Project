\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is increasingly adopted for aligning large language models, yet its three-phase training loop---policy rollouts, reward scoring, and gradient updates---is compute-intensive, with matrix multiplications accounting for $\sim$80--90\% of total compute.
Current accelerators apply fixed numerical precision uniformly across all operations, missing opportunities to reduce energy in phases that tolerate lower precision.
We investigate whether \textbf{layer-adaptive Microscaling (MX) format quantization} can reduce the energy consumption of GEMM operations during RLHF training on an FPGA, compared to an INT8 baseline.
Using Qwen2.5-0.5B trained with PPO on 1,200 Anthropic HH-RLHF preference pairs, we offload all GEMM operations to an AWS F2 FPGA (Xilinx VU9P) via AXI interface.
Our INT8 baseline measurement reveals that 99.99\% of per-matmul latency is consumed by PCIe data transfer (148,656 cycles) versus only 15 cycles for on-chip computation, directly motivating data compression via MX formats.
We conduct per-layer sensitivity profiling across all 24 transformer blocks, finding that \textbf{$\sim$80\% of layers tolerate MXFP4} (4-bit) quantization with $<$2\% perplexity increase, while MLP layers in blocks 2, 3, 21, and 23 require MXFP8 fallback.
We define four quantization policies (Conservative, Balanced, Aggressive, Phase-Adaptive) that assign per-layer, per-phase precision, and implement a dual-precision MXFP4/MXFP8 processing element in SystemC/HLS.
MXFP4 offers a projected \textbf{50\% bandwidth reduction} for weight transfer, translating to an estimated \textbf{25\% energy reduction} when applied to tolerant layers across the RLHF workload.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Post-training alignment via RLHF \citep{ouyang2022training} is expanding beyond frontier labs as enterprises fine-tune models for specialised verticals: legal, healthcare, finance, and customer service.
Unlike single-pass inference or supervised fine-tuning, RLHF requires three distinct compute phases per training step: (1)~policy rollouts (autoregressive generation), (2)~reward model inference, and (3)~PPO gradient updates.
Matrix multiplications account for $\sim$80--90\% of total compute across these phases, yet each phase has fundamentally different precision requirements: rollouts are tolerant to approximation (they only need ``good enough'' token sampling), reward scoring requires moderate fidelity, and gradient updates are precision-sensitive due to accumulation of small parameter changes.

Current FPGA and GPU accelerators apply fixed precision---typically FP16, FP8, or INT8---uniformly across all operations and phases, leaving significant energy savings on the table.
The Microscaling (MX) format family \citep{rouhani2023mxformat} provides a principled framework for fine-grained quantization through \emph{group scaling}: a shared exponent governs a block of $k$ values (typically $k{=}8$ or $k{=}16$), preserving dynamic range while reducing per-element bit width.
MXFP8 (E4M3 with group scale) matches FP16 quality on most workloads; MXFP4 (E2M1) halves the weight storage at the cost of slightly reduced precision.

We ask: \textbf{can we exploit the phase-specific and layer-specific precision tolerance of RLHF to reduce FPGA energy consumption by adaptively assigning MX formats?}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/rlhf_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{The three phases of RLHF training with their precision tolerance. Rollouts tolerate MXFP4; gradient updates require FP16 or MXFP8.}
\label{fig:rlhf-pipeline}
\end{figure}

Our approach has three stages:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{INT8 FPGA Baseline}: Establish energy and performance reference using the Lab~1 16$\times$16 INT8 PE core on AWS F2, measuring per-matmul cycle counts and energy.
    \item \textbf{Layer Sensitivity Profiling}: Quantize each of the 169 linear layers in Qwen2.5-0.5B independently to MXFP4 and MXFP8 (with group sizes 8 and 16), measuring perplexity delta. Layers with $<$2\% degradation are MXFP4-tolerant.
    \item \textbf{Policy-Driven Quantization}: Define four MX format policies (A--D) that assign per-layer, per-phase precision. Implement a dual-precision MXFP4/MXFP8 datapath in SystemC and HLS, targeting AWS F2 deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{FPGA baseline characterisation}: first detailed cycle-level breakdown of RLHF GEMM offload on AWS F2, revealing the 99.99\% PCIe bottleneck.
    \item \textbf{Per-layer MX sensitivity matrix}: comprehensive profiling of all 169 layers in Qwen2.5-0.5B under MXFP4/MXFP8 with two group sizes, showing $\sim$80\% MXFP4 tolerance.
    \item \textbf{Four quantization policies}: Conservative (all MXFP8), Balanced (MXFP4 for tolerant layers), Aggressive (MXFP4 everywhere), and Phase-Adaptive (format varies by RLHF phase).
    \item \textbf{Dual-precision hardware}: SystemC reference model and HLS datapath for MXFP4/MXFP8 PE with runtime mode switching.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\textbf{RLHF training.} RLHF \citep{ouyang2022training, christiano2017deep} aligns language models with human preferences by training a reward model on pairwise comparisons and then optimising the policy via Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) \citep{schulman2017ppo}.
Each PPO step involves: (1)~generating candidate responses (rollout), (2)~scoring them with the reward model, and (3)~computing policy gradients.
The TRL library \citep{vonwerra2022trl} provides a standard implementation.

\textbf{Microscaling (MX) formats.} The MX format specification \citep{rouhani2023mxformat} defines a family of block-scaled number formats.
A group of $k$ values shares a single exponent (the \emph{scale factor}), reducing per-element storage:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{MXFP8} (E4M3): 8-bit elements with 4-bit exponent and 3-bit mantissa, plus shared group scale. Matches FP16 on most inference workloads.
    \item \textbf{MXFP4} (E2M1): 4-bit elements with 2-bit exponent and 1-bit mantissa. Halves bandwidth versus MXFP8 at the cost of reduced precision. Suitable for inference-like computations.
\end{itemize}
Microsoft's \texttt{mx-pytorch} library provides reference quantisation/dequantisation kernels for PyTorch, enabling software simulation of MX formats.
Figure~\ref{fig:mx-formats} compares the bit layouts of INT8, MXFP8, and MXFP4.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/mx_format_comparison.pdf}
\caption{Bit-level comparison of INT8 (baseline), MXFP8 (E4M3), and MXFP4 (E2M1). MX formats add a shared group scale; MXFP4 halves the per-element storage.}
\label{fig:mx-formats}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Quantization for training.} Mixed-precision training with FP16 \citep{micikevicius2018mixed} is standard practice.
Recent work extends this to FP8 training \citep{micikevicius2022fp8}, and NVIDIA's H100 GPU provides native FP8 tensor cores.
However, sub-8-bit training (e.g., MXFP4 for gradients) remains challenging due to accumulation errors.
Our approach is complementary: we apply aggressive quantization only to phases and layers that tolerate it, preserving higher precision where needed.

\textbf{FPGA acceleration of ML.} FPGAs offer energy-efficient matrix multiplication through custom datapaths and reduced-precision arithmetic \citep{guo2017angel, wang2021fpga}.
The AWS F2 platform (Xilinx VU9P) provides cloud-accessible FPGAs with PCIe Gen3 x16 connectivity.
Lab~1 of CS217 provides a baseline 16$\times$16 INT8 PE core that we extend with MX format support.

\textbf{Layer-adaptive quantization.} Prior work on mixed-precision quantization for inference \citep{dong2019hawq, wang2019haq} uses Hessian-based or reinforcement-learning-based policies to assign bit widths per layer.
Our work differs by: (1)~targeting RLHF training rather than inference, (2)~exploiting phase-specific tolerance within the training loop, and (3)~using MX group scaling rather than per-tensor quantization.

\section{Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{System Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:system-overview} illustrates our system architecture.
The host CPU runs the full RLHF training loop (tokenisation, sampling, KL divergence, PPO updates) using PyTorch and TRL.
All GEMM operations are intercepted and offloaded to the FPGA via a tiling and offload layer that decomposes arbitrary matrix sizes into 16$\times$16 tiles, sends them to the FPGA via AXI, and reassembles results.
An adaptive controller reads the per-layer quantization policy (JSON) and sets the FPGA PE's precision mode (MXFP4, MXFP8, or INT8) before each operation.

\subsection{Model and Dataset}

\textbf{Policy and Reward Model:} Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct \citep{qwen2025qwen25}, a 0.5B-parameter transformer with 24 layers, each containing 7 linear projections (Q, K, V, O in attention; gate, up, down in the MLP), plus the LM head---totalling 169 quantizable layers.

\textbf{Dataset:} A curated subset of Anthropic HH-RLHF \citep{bai2022training}, consisting of 1,200 preference pairs (1,000 train / 200 eval), with sequence length capped at 512 tokens.
The dataset is hosted on HuggingFace (\texttt{hivamoh/cs217-rlhf-dataset}).

\textbf{Training:} 50 PPO steps with batch size 8 using the TRL library.
Each training sample requires 170 matmuls per forward pass ($24 \times 7 + 2$) and 340 per sample including the backward pass, yielding 136,000 total FPGA matmul calls for the full training run.


\subsection{INT8 FPGA Baseline}
\label{sec:fpga-baseline}

We deploy the Lab~1 16$\times$16 INT8 matrix multiply accelerator on AWS F2 (Xilinx VU9P, 250~MHz, $\sim$35W).
Each matmul call transfers 336 bytes over PCIe: 256B weights ($16{\times}16$ INT8), 16B input vector, and 64B output.

\textbf{Measurement protocol:} 10-iteration average of cycle counts, separated into data transfer cycles (PCIe host$\leftrightarrow$FPGA) and compute cycles (on-chip MAC).
Energy is computed as $E = P \times T$ where $P{=}35$W (XPE estimate) and $T$ is measured wall-clock time.


\subsection{Layer Sensitivity Profiling}
\label{sec:sensitivity-profiling}

We profile each of the 169 linear layers in Qwen2.5-0.5B independently using Microsoft's \texttt{mx-pytorch} library.
For each layer $\ell$, we:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Quantise only $\ell$ to MXFP4 or MXFP8 (with group size $k \in \{8, 16\}$), keeping all other layers at FP16.
    \item Evaluate perplexity on a held-out calibration set.
    \item Compute $\Delta_\ell = (\text{PPL}_\ell - \text{PPL}_{\text{base}}) / \text{PPL}_{\text{base}} \times 100$.
\end{enumerate}
A layer is \emph{MXFP4-tolerant} if $|\Delta_\ell| < 2\%$ for all group sizes.
The output is a \emph{sensitivity matrix}: rows are layers, columns are format $\times$ group size combinations, and cells are perplexity deltas.


\subsection{Quantization Policies}
\label{sec:policies}

From the sensitivity matrix, we define four policies that assign precision per layer and per RLHF phase:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Policy} & \textbf{Rollout} & \textbf{Reward} & \textbf{Gradient} \\
\midrule
A -- Conservative   & MXFP8 all layers   & MXFP8 all layers   & FP16 all layers \\
B -- Balanced       & MXFP4 tolerant, MXFP8 rest & MXFP4 tolerant, MXFP8 rest & FP16 all layers \\
C -- Aggressive     & MXFP4 all layers   & MXFP4 all layers   & MXFP4 tolerant, MXFP8 sensitive \\
D -- Phase-Adaptive & MXFP4 tolerant     & MXFP8 all layers   & MXFP8 tolerant, FP16 sensitive \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Four MX format policies. All use group size $k{=}8$.}
\label{tab:policies}
\end{table}

Policy~B is the primary recommendation: it applies MXFP4 to the $\sim$80\% of layers that tolerate it during rollout and reward phases, achieving substantial bandwidth reduction with no quality degradation, while preserving FP16 for gradients.
Policy~D adds phase-awareness, using more conservative precision for reward scoring (which affects policy gradient signal quality) while being aggressive during rollouts.


\subsection{Hardware Design}
\label{sec:hardware}

\subsubsection{SystemC Reference Model}

We implement a cycle-accurate SystemC reference model for the dual-precision MX datapath:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{mx\_types.h}: Defines \texttt{PrecisionMode} enum (MXFP4/MXFP8/INT8), \texttt{MiniFloatSpec} for E4M3 and E2M1 formats.
    \item \texttt{group\_scaler.h}: Group scaling logic---finds the max exponent within a group of $k$ values and uses it as the shared scale.
    Quantise: clamp the mantissa to the target width; dequantise: reconstruct the full-precision value from element bits plus group scale.
    \item \texttt{mxfp\_pe.h}: A single MX processing element (multiply-accumulate) that accepts two MX-encoded operands and a precision mode.
    \item \texttt{mx\_datapath.h}: Orchestrates a $16{\times}16$ GEMM using the PE array, with runtime mode switching between MXFP4, MXFP8, and INT8.
\end{itemize}
Testbenches verify bit-exact agreement with the PyTorch reference outputs from \texttt{mx-pytorch}.

\subsubsection{HLS Datapath}

The HLS implementation (\texttt{Datapath.h}) extends the Lab~1 INT8 PE core with MX format decode:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{DecodeMXByte}: Extracts sign, exponent, and mantissa from an MXFP8 (E4M3) or MXFP4 (E2M1) encoded byte, applies the group scale, and produces an INT16 fixed-point value compatible with the existing MAC pipeline.
    \item \texttt{PrecisionMode} register: The host sets the PE's precision mode before each GEMM tile via an AXI control register.
    \item The existing INT8 multiply-accumulate path is preserved as a fallback.
\end{itemize}
This design allows \textbf{zero-overhead mode switching} between INT8 and MX formats on a per-tile basis, enabling the adaptive controller to implement any of the four policies without FPGA reconfiguration.

The key finding is stark: \textbf{computation takes 15 cycles; data transfer takes 148,641 cycles}.
The FPGA is not compute-bound---it is overwhelmingly \emph{memory-bandwidth-bound}.
Ideal PCIe Gen3 x16 bandwidth ($\sim$12~GB/s) predicts $\sim$28~$\mu$s transfer time for 336 bytes, but we observe 594.56~$\mu$s due to AXI protocol overhead, host memory copies, and SRAM serialisation.

This bottleneck directly motivates MX format compression: MXFP4 halves the weight payload from 256B to 128B (plus a small group-scale overhead of $16{\times}16/k$ bytes), yielding a near-proportional reduction in transfer cycles.

\label{sec:systemc-validation}

Before HLS synthesis, we validate the dual-precision MX datapath using the SystemC reference model.
The testbench evaluates quantization accuracy, MAC operations, GEMM correctness, and mode switching safety across multiple random seeds.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}llcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Operation} & \textbf{Mean Error} & \textbf{Max Error} \\
\midrule

MXFP4 (E2M1) & MAC (dot product)  & 4.7\%  & 5.0\% \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{SystemC datapath accuracy validation. Errors are normalized (MAC) or absolute (GEMM). All values are within testbench thresholds (8\% MAC, 85\% GEMM for MXFP4).}
\label{tab:systemc-validation}
\end{table}

\textbf{Experimental setup:} 40 simulation runs across 20 random seeds (100--119), each with two test profiles: \emph{baseline} (300 MAC trials, 20 GEMM trials) and \emph{stress} (1,200 MAC trials, 80 GEMM trials).
All runs use group size $k{=}8$.

\textbf{Results:} All 40 runs passed with 100\% success rate.
Key observations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{MXFP8 is highly accurate}: MAC errors under 1\%, GEMM errors under 4\%---comparable to the FP16 baseline.
    \item \textbf{MXFP4 has $\sim$7$\times$ higher error than MXFP8}: expected given the reduced mantissa width (1-bit vs.\ 3-bit).
    \item \textbf{Consistent across seeds}: low variance ($<$0.1\% standard deviation) confirms stable datapath behavior.
    \item \textbf{Group size 8 $\approx$ group size 16}: both yield similar error profiles, validating our choice of $k{=}8$.
    \item \textbf{Mode switching verified}: the datapath correctly blocks operations until pipeline flush after precision mode change.
\end{itemize}

These results confirm that the SystemC reference model correctly implements MXFP4 and MXFP8 arithmetic with accuracy sufficient for the layer sensitivity thresholds established in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity-results}.
The HLS datapath is synthesized from this validated reference.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The PCIe Bottleneck}

The most striking finding is the 99.99\% PCIe overhead.
For each 16$\times$16 matmul, the FPGA moves 336 bytes (256B weights + 16B input + 64B output).
The on-chip computation is 15 cycles at 250~MHz (60~ns), but the round-trip PCIe transfer requires 594.56~$\mu$s---nearly 10,000$\times$ longer.

This has broader implications beyond our project:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Compression $>$ raw FLOPS}: For small-tile offload architectures, reducing data volume has far greater impact than faster compute.
    \item \textbf{Batch amortisation}: Transferring larger tiles or batches of tiles can amortise the per-transfer overhead. A 64$\times$64 tile would increase compute from 15 to $\sim$960 cycles while only $\sim$4$\times$ increasing transfer volume.
    \item \textbf{On-chip caching}: Keeping weights on FPGA SRAM between operations eliminates repeated transfers for the same layer.
\end{enumerate}

MXFP4 directly addresses the first point: halving weight precision from 8 bits to 4 bits reduces the dominant cost component by $\sim$50\%.


\subsection{Sensitivity Patterns}

The sensitivity matrix reveals a clear architectural pattern: attention layers (Q/K/V/O projections) are robust to aggressive quantization, while MLP layers show position-dependent sensitivity.

\textbf{MLP sensitivity at network boundaries}: blocks 2--3 (early) and 21--23 (late) contain the sensitive MLP layers.
Early layers transform embedding-space representations into the model's internal feature space; late layers shape the output logit distribution.
Both operations involve large dynamic ranges that MXFP4's 2-bit exponent cannot fully capture.
Middle layers (4--20) perform more gradual feature refinement within an established dynamic range, making them MXFP4-tolerant.

\textbf{Group size has minimal impact}: switching from $k{=}8$ to $k{=}16$ slightly increases perplexity deltas (larger groups share a less precise scale) but does not change the tolerant/sensitive classification for any layer.
This suggests that $k{=}8$ is sufficient, and the small overhead of more frequent group scales is justified.


\subsection{Phase-Specific Tolerance}

The three RLHF phases have fundamentally different precision requirements:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Rollout (most tolerant)}: Autoregressive generation samples from a softmax distribution. Small numerical errors in logits are absorbed by temperature scaling and top-$k$/top-$p$ filtering. MXFP4 is safe for all layers.
    \item \textbf{Reward scoring (moderately tolerant)}: The reward model outputs a scalar score per response. Small perturbations in intermediate representations are compressed to a single value, providing natural noise robustness. MXFP8 is safe; MXFP4 is safe for tolerant layers.
    \item \textbf{Gradient updates (sensitive)}: PPO gradients accumulate over many samples. Quantization noise in gradients can compound across steps, leading to training instability. FP16 or MXFP8 is recommended; MXFP4 is risky except for the most tolerant layers.
\end{itemize}

This hierarchy motivates Policy~D (Phase-Adaptive), which applies the most aggressive quantization to rollouts, moderate quantization to reward scoring, and conservative precision to gradients.


\subsection{Comparison with GPU Baselines}

While our FPGA measurements establish the energy profile for offloaded RLHF, production GPU deployments differ substantially:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item NVIDIA H100 provides native FP8 tensor cores, eliminating the need for explicit format conversion.
    \item GPU memory bandwidth ($\sim$3~TB/s for H100 HBM3) is orders of magnitude higher than PCIe Gen3, making the transfer bottleneck less pronounced.
    \item However, GPU power consumption ($\sim$700W) is $\sim$20$\times$ higher than our FPGA (35W), making energy per operation a more nuanced comparison.
\end{itemize}

The FPGA platform serves as a \emph{controlled research environment}: it enables precise, isolated energy measurement not feasible on production GPUs, where power monitoring tools lack the granularity to attribute energy to individual matmul operations.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Practical Implications}

Our results suggest a practical deployment strategy for RLHF energy optimisation:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Profile once, deploy everywhere}: The sensitivity matrix for a given model architecture is stable across datasets. Profile the model once on a small calibration set, then apply the resulting policy to all RLHF training runs.
    \item \textbf{Start with Policy~A}: MXFP8 everywhere is universally safe ($<$0.16\% quality impact) and provides modest energy savings via more efficient MAC operations.
    \item \textbf{Graduate to Policy~B for production}: Apply MXFP4 to the $\sim$80\% tolerant layers for $\sim$20\% energy reduction with negligible quality risk.
    \item \textbf{Use Policy~D for maximum savings}: Phase-adaptive precision offers the best energy--quality tradeoff but requires runtime mode switching, which our HLS design supports.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Scale}: Qwen2.5-0.5B is small by current standards. Larger models (7B+) may show different sensitivity patterns, though the attention-tolerant/MLP-boundary-sensitive pattern is architecturally motivated.
    \item \textbf{PCIe overhead}: The 99.99\% transfer bottleneck is an artefact of the 16$\times$16 tile size and single-operation offload. Batched transfers or on-chip weight caching would substantially reduce this overhead, changing the energy breakdown.
    \item \textbf{Training length}: 50 PPO steps is sufficient for demonstrating the approach but insufficient for production RLHF alignment. Longer training runs (1,000+ steps) are needed to verify that quantization noise does not accumulate.
    \item \textbf{Simulation vs.\ measurement}: Energy projections for MX policies are based on cycle-count extrapolation from the INT8 baseline, not direct FPGA measurements with the MX datapath deployed.
    \item \textbf{Single model architecture}: Sensitivity patterns may differ for architectures without grouped-query attention or with different FFN structures (e.g., MoE models).
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have investigated layer-adaptive MX format quantization as a strategy for reducing FPGA energy consumption during RLHF training.

\noindent\textbf{Key findings:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{PCIe transfer dominates}: 99.99\% of per-matmul cycles are spent on data transfer, making bandwidth compression the primary lever for energy reduction.
    \item \textbf{$\sim$80\% of layers tolerate MXFP4}: Attention projections are universally safe; MLP layers in early (2--3) and late (21--23) blocks are the only sensitive components.
    \item \textbf{MXFP8 is universally safe}: Maximum 0.16\% perplexity impact across all 169 layers.
    \item \textbf{Phase-aware quantization is viable}: Rollouts tolerate MXFP4; gradients require at least MXFP8.
    \item \textbf{Projected 20--25\% energy savings}: Policy~B (Balanced) achieves $\sim$20\% energy reduction; Policy~C (Aggressive) reaches $\sim$25\%.
    \item \textbf{RLHF quality preserved}: INT8 baseline achieves 21\% reward improvement and 3.3\% perplexity reduction, confirming viable FPGA-offloaded training.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Future work:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Deploy MX datapath on FPGA}: Synthesise the HLS dual-precision PE, generate the bitstream, and measure actual energy under policies A--D.
    \item \textbf{Scale to larger models}: Validate sensitivity patterns on 7B+ parameter models (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B).
    \item \textbf{Extend training runs}: Run 1,000+ PPO steps to verify long-horizon stability under MX quantization.
    \item \textbf{Benchmark against GPU FP8}: Compare FPGA MX energy profiles with H100 FP8 tensor core baselines.
    \item \textbf{On-chip weight caching}: Implement SRAM-resident weight tiles to amortise PCIe transfer overhead across multiple forward/backward passes.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic policy adaptation}: Learn per-layer precision assignments online during training, adjusting based on gradient magnitude statistics.
\end{enumerate}
